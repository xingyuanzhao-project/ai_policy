A.B. 390

ASSEMBLY BILL NO. 390–ASSEMBLYMEMBERS

GONZÁLEZ; D’SILVA AND JACKSON

MARCH 11, 2025

Referred to Committee on Commerce and Labor

SUMMARY—Enacts provisions relating to task workers. (BDR 53-547)

FISCAL NOTE: Effect on Local Government: May have Fiscal Impact.

Effect on the State: Yes.

AN ACT relating to labor; authorizing collective bargaining between task workers and task delivery platform providers; setting forth procedures and requirements governing such collective bargaining; requiring a task delivery platform provider to provide certain information to users and task workers; requiring a task delivery platform provider to ensure that a task worker is paid an amount not less than the minimum wage for each hour worked performing tasks; setting forth certain requirements and restrictions for contracts between a task delivery platform provider and a task worker; renaming and expanding the duties of the Government Employee-Management Relations Board; providing a penalty; and providing other matters properly relating thereto.

Legislative Counsel’s Digest:

This bill enacts provisions relating to task workers. Section 11 of this bill defines “task worker” to mean a natural person who has entered into a contract with a task delivery platform provider to receive connections to users in need of the performance of a specific task through the task delivery platform of the provider. Section 9 of this bill defines “task delivery platform” to mean an Internet website, online service or mobile application that: (1) connects users in need of the performance of a specific task to a task worker who can perform the task; and (2) uses algorithms or automated systems for certain purposes. Section 10 of this bill defines “task delivery platform provider” or “provider” to mean a person who operates a task delivery platform.

Sections 13-35 of this bill authorize collective bargaining between task workers, through a task worker organization certified as an exclusive bargaining representative, and providers, and set forth procedures and requirements governing such collective bargaining. Section 14 provides that task workers have the right to: (1) organize, form, join or assist task worker organizations, engage in collective bargaining through exclusive bargaining representatives and engage in other concerted activities; and (2) refrain from engaging in such activities.

Existing law creates the Government Employee-Management Relations Board and sets forth the powers and duties of the Board concerning collective bargaining between public employees and public employers. (Chapter 288 of NRS) Sections 13-35 and 43-47, 49 and 50 of this bill expand the powers and duties of the Board to include various powers and duties relating to collective bargaining between task workers and providers. Sections 41, 48 and 49 of this bill change the name of the Board to the Labor Relations Board to conform to this change in powers and duties.

Section 15 sets forth certain prohibited practices for task delivery platform providers and task worker organizations, including, among others, the refusal to collectively bargain in good faith concerning the compensation, hours, procedures for the adjustment of grievances and other terms and conditions of work. Section 16 sets forth procedures for the Board to hear and decide complaints alleging that a party committed such a prohibited practice.

Section 17 requires each provider to submit certain quarterly reports to the Board concerning the task workers with whom the provider has contracted. Section 17 additionally requires the Board to maintain a list of each active task worker based on the information provided in such reports.

Section 18 requires the Board to establish by regulation industry-wide bargaining units consisting of groups of task workers who have a sufficient community of interest appropriate for representation by a task worker organization for the purpose of collective bargaining.

Section 19 sets forth procedures by which a task worker organization may be designated as a recognized bargaining representative of a bargaining unit by the Board. Sections 20 and 21 set forth procedures by which a task worker organization may be certified as the exclusive bargaining representative for a bargaining unit. Section 23 provides that a task worker organization that has been certified as an exclusive bargaining representative remains the exclusive bargaining representative until replaced or decertified in accordance with the procedures set forth in sections 23 and 29. Section 22 sets forth procedures for the conduct of certain elections ordered by the Board relating to the representation of task workers.

Section 24 grants an exclusive bargaining representative the exclusive right to engage in collective bargaining with providers who have contracted with task workers within the bargaining unit represented by the exclusive bargaining representative.

Section 25 establishes procedures for the deduction from the compensation due to a task worker of amounts for membership dues in a task worker organization.

Sections 26-31 set forth procedures and requirements governing the collective bargaining process to reach a recommended agreement between an exclusive bargaining representative of a bargaining unit and the providers who have contracted with task workers within the bargaining unit, including procedures for mediation and arbitration.

Section 32 provides that any recommended agreement reached by the parties or the decision of an arbitrator does not become effective until approved by the Labor Commissioner. Section 32 authorizes the Labor Commissioner to approve, with or without modification, or reject any recommended agreement or decision of an arbitrator. If the Labor Commissioner approves an agreement or decision, section 32 requires the Labor Commissioner to establish effective and expiration dates for the agreement or decision. Section 33 provides that such an agreement does not preempt or supersede any statute or regulation setting forth labor standards that provide greater benefits or protections to a task worker.

Section 34 provides for judicial review of certain determinations and decisions of the Board and Labor Commissioner made pursuant to sections 13-35. Section 35 authorizes the Board and the Labor Commissioner to adopt certain regulations.

The Nevada Unfair Trade Practice Act sets forth various activities that constitute a contract, combination or conspiracy in restraint of trade and authorizes the Attorney General to investigate and take certain actions against persons who engage in such activities, which may include, without limitation, criminal prosecution and the imposition of civil penalties. (Chapter 598A of NRS) Under the state action immunity doctrine, private parties engaged in activities that would otherwise violate federal antitrust laws may be immune from such laws if the activity is undertaken pursuant to a “clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed . . . state policy” and the policy is “actively supervised by the State itself.” (California Retail Liquor Dealers Ass’n v. Midcal Aluminum, Inc., 445 U.S. 97, 105 (1980)(internal quotation marks omitted)) Section 13 expresses the intention of the Legislature to exempt activities authorized pursuant to sections 13-35 that may otherwise be constrained by State antitrust laws from such laws and to provide immunity for such activities from federal antitrust laws through the state action immunity doctrine. Section 51 of this bill exempts activities authorized by sections 13-35 from the provisions of the Nevada Unfair Trade Practice Act.

Section 36 of this bill requires a task delivery platform provider to: (1) make certain disclosures to users of the task delivery platform of the provider; and (2) provide a task worker with whom the provider has contracted regular reports concerning the compensation of the task worker.

Existing law requires an employer to pay an employee a wage of not less than a certain minimum wage for each hour the employee works. (Nev. Const. Art. 15, § 16; NRS 608.250) Existing law requires the Labor Commissioner to administer and enforce the provisions of existing law governing the minimum wage and makes it a misdemeanor for a person to violate those provisions. (NRS 608.270, 608.290) Existing law authorizes an employee who has been paid by an employer a lesser amount than the minimum wage to bring a civil action against the employer. (NRS 608.260) Sections 37, 39 and 40 of this bill: (1) require a task delivery platform provider to ensure that a task worker with whom the provider has contracted is paid for each hour the task worker works in performing tasks through the task delivery platform of the provider an amount that is not less than the minimum wage; (2) authorizes a task worker to bring a civil action against a provider who violates that requirement; (3) provides for the administration and enforcement of that requirement by the Labor Commissioner; and (4) makes it a misdemeanor to violate that requirement.

Section 38 of this bill sets forth certain requirements and restrictions for contracts between a task worker and a task delivery platform provider.

Sections 3-12 of this bill define words and terms relating to task workers.

THE PEOPLE OF THE STATE OF NEVADA, REPRESENTED IN SENATE AND ASSEMBLY, DO ENACT AS FOLLOWS:

Section 1. Title 53 of NRS is hereby amended by adding thereto a new chapter to consist of the provisions set forth as sections 2 to 38, inclusive, of this act.

Sec. 2. As used in this chapter, unless the context otherwise requires, the words and terms defined in sections 3 to 12, inclusive, of this act have the meanings ascribed to them in those sections.

Sec. 3. “Active task worker” means a task worker designated by the Board as an active task worker who appears on the most recent list of active task workers maintained by the Board pursuant to section 17 of this act.

Sec. 4. “Bargaining unit” means a group of task workers that the Board has established as a bargaining unit pursuant to section 18 of this act.

Sec. 5. “Board” means the Labor Relations Board created by NRS 288.080.

Sec. 6. 1. “Company union” means any committee, employee representation plan, association of workers or other organization having as one of its purposes the improvement of the terms and conditions of work for task workers or the adjustment of grievances of task workers with task delivery platform providers:

(a) That a provider has initiated or created or whose initiation or creation was suggested or participated in by a provider;

(b) Whose governing rules, management, operations or elections a provider has participated in; or

(c) That is controlled, dominated, maintained or financed, in whole or in part, by a provider.

2. The term does not include a task worker organization that:

(a) Has negotiated or otherwise been granted the right to designate task workers to be released with pay by a provider for the purpose of providing representational services on behalf of task workers represented by the task worker organization; or

(b) Is authorized by a provider to meet with task workers at the premises of the provider in the course of providing representational services on behalf of task workers represented by the task worker organization.

Sec. 7. “Exclusive bargaining representative” means a task worker organization that has been certified as the exclusive bargaining representative of a bargaining unit pursuant to section 20 or 22 of this act.

Sec. 8. “Task” means a short-term job, project, assignment or other piece of work:

1. That is performed by a task worker on-demand at the request of a user through a task delivery platform; and

2. For which the task worker receives compensation through the task delivery platform.

Sec. 9. “Task delivery platform” means an Internet website, online service or mobile application that:

1. Connects users in need of the performance of a specific task to a task worker who can perform the task; and

2. Uses algorithms or automated systems to:

(a) Connect users with task workers;

(b) Determine the rate of pay for a task worker for the performance of a specific task through the platform; and

(c) Evaluate the performance of a task worker.

Sec. 10. “Task delivery platform provider” or “provider” means a person who operates a task delivery platform.

Sec. 11. “Task worker” means a natural person who has entered into a contract with a task delivery platform provider to receive connections to users in need of the performance of a specific task through the task delivery platform of the provider. The term does not include a person who is an employee, as defined in 29 U.S.C. § 152(3).

Sec. 12. “Task worker organization” means an organization of any kind, other than a company union, having as one of its purposes the improvement of the terms and conditions of work for task workers or the adjustment of grievances of task workers with task delivery platform providers.

Sec. 13. The Legislature hereby finds and declares that:

1. Task workers have become a prevalent and important part of the economy of this State.

2. Task workers often suffer from inadequate pay and benefits, as well as irregular or inadequate working hours.

3. It is in the best interest of the State to provide task workers the opportunity to self-organize, designate representatives of their own choosing and to bargain collectively to obtain sustainable compensation, benefits and working conditions, subject to the approval and ongoing supervision of the State.

4. It is the policy of this State to exempt activities authorized pursuant to sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act, including, without limitation, the formation of task worker organizations and industry associations for the purpose of collective bargaining between task workers and task delivery platform providers on an industry-wide basis, that might otherwise be constrained by State antitrust laws from such laws and to provide immunity for such activities from federal antitrust laws through the state action immunity doctrine.

Sec. 14. 1. Task workers have the right to:

(a) Organize, form, join or assist task worker organizations, engage in collective bargaining through exclusive bargaining representatives and engage in other concerted activities; and

(b) Refrain from engaging in such activity.

2. Collective bargaining entails a mutual obligation of a task delivery platform provider and an exclusive bargaining representative to meet at reasonable times and to bargain in good faith with respect to compensation, hours, procedures for the adjustment of grievances and other terms and conditions of work in an attempt to reach a recommended agreement to be submitted to the Labor Commissioner pursuant to section 32 of this act.

3. In addition to any other information required pursuant to sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act, a task worker shall furnish to an exclusive bargaining representative data that is maintained in the ordinary course of business and which is relevant to the subjects described in subsection 2. This subsection shall not be construed to require a provider to furnish to the exclusive bargaining representative any advice or training received by representatives of the provider concerning collective bargaining.

4. Nothing in the provisions of sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act prohibit a task worker from conferring with a task delivery platform provider at any time concerning any matter, so long as such a conference does not involve an attempt by the provider to interfere with or restrain the exercise of any right granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

Sec. 15. 1. It is a prohibited practice for a task delivery platform provider to:

(a) Fail or refuse to provide the Board, an exclusive bargaining representative or other task worker organization any information required by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

(b) Refuse to collectively bargain in good faith concerning the subjects described in subsection 2 of section 14 of this act.

(c) Engage in a lockout of task workers.

(d) Conduct surveillance, directly or through another person, of a task worker or a task worker organization concerning activities of such persons in the exercise of the rights granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

(e) Except as otherwise required or authorized by the provisions of sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act or any regulation adopted pursuant thereto, interfere with the formation, existence or administration of any task worker organization, including, without limitation, by:

(1) Contributing money or other support to any task worker organization.

(2) Participating in, assisting, supervising or controlling;

(I) The initiation or creation of any task worker organization; or

(II) The meetings, management, operation, elections, formulation or amendment of the constitution, rules or policies of any such organization.

(3) Offering incentives to task workers to join any task worker organization.

(4) Donating free services, materials, office or meeting space or anything else of value for use by any task worker organization, except that provisions of this subparagraph do not prohibit a provider from allowing:

(I) A task worker to perform work relating to the rights granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act during working hours without loss of time or pay; or

(II) An agent of an exclusive bargaining representative to meet with task workers in the bargaining unit represented by the representative at the premises of the provider.

(f) Require a task worker to join any company union or task worker organization.

(g) Require a task worker to refrain from forming, joining or assisting a task worker organization of his or her own choosing.

(h) Encourage membership in a company union or discourage membership in a task worker organization by discrimination with respect to the hiring or in any term or condition of work.

(i) Discharge or otherwise discriminate against a task worker because the task worker has signed or filed any affidavit, petition or complaint or given any information or testimony pursuant to sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

(j) Distribute or circulate any blacklist of persons exercising any right granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act or of the members of a task worker organization, or inform any person of the exercise by any person of such a right or of the membership of any person in a task worker organization for the purpose of preventing persons so blacklisted or so named from obtaining or retaining opportunities for remuneration.

(k) Take any other action which interferes with, restrains or coerces task workers in the exercise of any right granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

2. It is a prohibited practice for a task worker organization to:

(a) Refuse to collectively bargain in good faith with a task delivery platform provider if the task worker organization is the exclusive bargaining representative for any task workers within the bargaining unit represented by the representative who have contracted with the provider.

(b) Refuse to provide the Board or a provider any information required by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

(c) Restrain or coerce any task worker in the exercise of any right granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act. Nothing in this paragraph prohibits a task worker organization from establishing internal rules governing the acquisition and retention of membership in the organization.

(d) Fail to fairly represent any task worker who the organization represents as an exclusive bargaining representative by acts or omissions that are arbitrary, discriminatory or in bad faith.

(e) Restrain or coerce a provider in the selection of any representative for the purpose of collective bargaining or the adjustment of grievances.

3. As used in this section, “lockout” means the refusal of a provider to allow a task worker normal access to the task delivery platform of the provider because of a dispute with task workers concerning the subjects described in subsection 2 of section 14 of this act. The term does not include the removal of a task worker from the task delivery platform of a provider for any reason other than for the exercise of any right granted by sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

Sec. 16. 1. To establish that a party committed a prohibited practice in violation of section 15 of this act, the party aggrieved by the practice must file a complaint with the Board in accordance with the procedures prescribed by the Board. Except as otherwise provided in subsection 2, the provisions of NRS 288.625 and 288.630 apply to a complaint filed pursuant to this section.

2. The affirmative relief that the Board may order pursuant to paragraph (b) of subsection 3 of NRS 288.625 or paragraph (b) of subsection 4 of NRS 288.630 concerning a complaint filed pursuant to this section includes, without limitation:

(a) The withdrawal of recognition of a task worker organization or decertification of a task worker organization as an exclusive bargaining representative;

(b) The awarding of back pay or other restoration of compensation to a task worker, without any reduction based on the interim earnings of the task worker or the failure of the task worker to earn interim earnings, consequential damages and an additional amount as liquidated damages equal to two times the amount of damages awarded;

(c) Requiring a provider to restore a task worker to the task delivery platform of the provider;

(d) Requiring a provider or task worker organization to provide specified information to a complainant; and

(e) Requiring a provider or task worker organization to engage in collective bargaining as required pursuant to sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

Sec. 17. 1. Not later than 14 days after the end of each calendar quarter, each task delivery platform provider shall provide to the Board a report that identifies each task worker who has contracted with the provider and completed five or more tasks in this State through the task delivery platform of the provider in the immediately preceding 6 months. The report must include, without limitation, the name, phone number, mailing address and electronic mail address of each such task worker.

2. The Board shall determine the median amount of tasks completed by task workers for all providers in this State in the immediately preceding 6 months based on the report submitted pursuant to subsection 1. The Board shall designate any task worker who completed more than the median amount of tasks during that period as an active task worker. The Board shall maintain and update after each calendar quarter a list of each active task worker.

Sec. 18. The Board shall establish by regulation industry-wide bargaining units for task workers consisting of groups of task workers who have a sufficient community of interest appropriate for representation by a task worker organization for the purpose of collective bargaining.

Sec. 19. 1. A task worker organization may submit a request to the Board to be designated as a recognized bargaining representative of a bargaining unit.

2. A request submitted pursuant to subsection 1 must be accompanied by evidence sufficient to show that at least 5 percent of the active task workers within the bargaining unit have authorized the task worker organization to act as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit. Such evidence may include, without limitation, cards, petitions or other evidence determined to be sufficient by the Board. Any such evidence must contain the signature of the task worker, which may be in electronic form, and must have been executed not more than 1 year before the date the evidence is submitted to the Board.

3. If the Board determines that the task worker organization that submitted a request pursuant to subsection 1 has been authorized by at least 5 percent of the active task workers within a bargaining unit to act as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit, the Board shall:

(a) Designate the task worker organization as a recognized bargaining representative for the bargaining unit;

(b) Order each provider who has contracted with a task worker within the bargaining unit to send a notice, in a form prescribed by the Board, to each such task worker indicating that the task worker organization seeks to represent the task worker; and

(c) Provide the task worker organization with a complete list of each active task worker in the bargaining unit that includes, without limitation, the name, phone number, mailing address and electronic mail address for each task worker. For 1 year after the date on which the Board designates the task worker organization as a recognized bargaining representative, the Board shall provide to the task worker organization an updated list following each calendar quarter.

4. For 6 months after the date on which the Board designates the task worker organization as a recognized bargaining representative, the Board shall not certify any task worker organization other than the task worker organization so designated as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit without an election ordered by the Board pursuant to section 20 or 21 of this act.

Sec. 20. 1. A task worker organization that wishes to be certified as the exclusive bargaining representative of a bargaining unit must submit to the Board a request for certification.

2. A request submitted pursuant to subsection 1 must be accompanied by:

(a) Evidence of the type described in subsection 2 of section 19 of this act sufficient to show that at least 25 percent of the active task workers within the bargaining unit have authorized the task worker organization to serve as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit; or

(b) Both:

(1) Evidence of the type described in subsection 2 of section 19 of this act sufficient to show that at least 5 percent of the active task workers within the bargaining unit have authorized the task worker organization to serve as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit; and

(2) A request to conduct an election among task workers within the bargaining unit.

3. Except as otherwise provided in section 19 of this act, if the Board determines that a task worker organization that submitted a request pursuant to paragraph (a) of subsection 2 has been authorized by:

(a) At least 25 percent but not more than 50 percent of the active task workers within a bargaining unit to act as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit, the Board shall certify the task worker organization as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit 7 days after the determination is made, unless, during the 7-day period, the certification is challenged in accordance with section 21 of this act.

(b) More than 50 percent of the active task workers within a bargaining unit to act as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit, certify the task worker organization as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit.

4. If the Board determines that a task worker organization that submitted a request pursuant to paragraph (b) of subsection 2 has been authorized by at least 5 percent of the active task workers within a bargaining unit to act as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit, the Board shall order an election to be conducted among the active task workers in the bargaining unit. The election must be conducted in accordance with section 22 of this act and any procedures and requirements established by the Board by regulation.

Sec. 21. 1. A task worker organization or task delivery platform provider may challenge the certification of a task worker organization described in paragraph (a) of subsection 3 of section 20 of this act as the exclusive bargaining representative of a bargaining unit if, during the 7-day period described in paragraph (a) of subsection 3 of section 20 of this act, the task worker organization or provider submits to the Board:

(a) For a task worker organization, evidence of the type described in subsection 2 of section 19 of this act sufficient to show that at least 25 percent of the active task workers within the bargaining unit have authorized the task worker organization to serve as the exclusive bargaining representative for the bargaining unit.

(b) For a provider, evidence sufficient to show that at least 25 percent of the active task workers within the bargaining unit do not wish to be represented by any task worker organization.

2. If the Board determines that a task worker organization or provider has submitted the evidence required by subsection 1, the Board shall order an election to be conducted among the active task workers within the bargaining unit. The election must be conducted in accordance with section 22 of this act and any procedures and requirements established by the Board by regulation.

Sec. 22. 1. If the Board orders an election within a bargaining unit pursuant to section 20 or 21 of this act, the Board shall order that each of the following be placed as a choice on the ballot for the election:

(a) If applicable, the task worker organization certified as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit;

(b) If applicable, the task worker organization requesting certification as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit pursuant to section 20 of this act;

(c) If applicable, the task worker organization challenging the certification pursuant to section 21 of this act; and

(d) A choice for “no representation.”

2. If a ballot for an election contains more than two choices and none of the choices on the ballot receives a majority of the votes cast at the initial election, the Board shall order a runoff election between the two choices on the ballot that received the highest number of votes at the initial election.

3. If the choice for “no representation” receives a majority of the votes cast at the election, the Board shall designate the bargaining unit as being without representation.

4. If a task worker organization receives a majority of the votes cast as the initial election or at any runoff election, the Board shall certify the task worker organization as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit.

Sec. 23. 1. A task worker organization that has been certified as the exclusive bargaining representative of a bargaining unit remains the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit until replaced or decertified.

2. Except as otherwise provided in subsection 4, an exclusive bargaining representative may be replaced by another task worker organization who submits a request for certification in accordance with the procedures set forth in section 20 of this act.

3. A provider or group of task workers may submit to the Board a request for the decertification of an exclusive bargaining representative. Such a request must be accompanied by evidence sufficient to show that at least 25 percent of active task workers within the bargaining unit do not wish to be represented by any task worker organization. Except as otherwise provided in subsection 4, if the Board determines that a task worker organization or provider has submitted such evidence, the Board shall order an election to be conducted among the active task workers within the bargaining unit. The election must be conducted in accordance with section 22 of this act and any procedures and requirements established by regulation of the Board.

4. Except as otherwise provided in section 29 of this act, an exclusive bargaining representative is not subject to replacement or decertification for the greater of:

(a) One year after the date upon which the task worker organization is certified as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit; or

(b) The period in which an agreement or decision approved by the Labor Commissioner pursuant to section 32 of this act is effective.

Sec. 24. An exclusive bargaining representative has the exclusive right to engage in collective bargaining with providers who have contracted with task workers within the bargaining unit represented by the exclusive bargaining representative for a recommended agreement concerning compensation, benefits, procedures for the adjustment of grievances and other terms and conditions of work for task workers within the bargaining unit.

Sec. 25. 1. An exclusive bargaining representative may submit to a provider a request signed by a task worker within the bargaining unit represented by the representative to deduct from the compensation due to the task worker an amount of money specified by the request and provide the money to the exclusive bargaining representative for membership dues.

2. A provider that receives a request submitted pursuant to subsection 1 shall commence making the required deductions and provide the money to the exclusive bargaining representative as soon as practicable, but in no case later than 30 days after the receipt of the request.

3. A provider shall not honor any request for deductions submitted by a task worker organization for a task worker within a bargaining unit other than the exclusive bargaining representative that represents the bargaining unit.

4. A request submitted pursuant to subsection 1 remains in effect until a task worker revokes his or her membership in the exclusive bargaining representative in accordance with the terms set forth in the request.

Sec. 26. 1. To commence the collective bargaining process, an exclusive bargaining representative shall provide written notice to the Board. Upon receipt of such notice, the Board shall notify each provider who has contracted with a task worker within the bargaining unit represented by the representative.

2. The exclusive bargaining representative and each provider notified pursuant to subsection 1 shall promptly commence negotiations to reach a recommended agreement concerning compensation, benefits, procedures for the adjustment of grievances and other terms and conditions of work. Such terms and conditions must include, without limitation:

(a) The circumstances under which a task worker may be removed from the task delivery platform of a provider; and

(b) Procedures for resolving a claim that alleges that a task worker was unjustifiably removed from the task delivery platform of a provider.

3. To facilitate the negotiations required by subsection 2, the providers subject to the negotiations may form an industry association and designate that association to negotiate on behalf of the providers.

Sec. 27. 1. Any recommended agreement resulting from negotiations required by section 26 of this act must be approved by the providers and exclusive bargaining representatives subject to the negotiations.

2. For the purposes of subsection 1, a recommended agreement shall be deemed to be approved by the providers subject to the negotiations if the recommended agreement is approved by:

(a) If the negotiations involve a single provider, the provider.

(b) If the negotiations involve multiple providers and the providers have formed an industry association pursuant to section 26 of this act, the industry association.

(c) If the negotiations involve multiple providers and the providers have not formed an industry association pursuant to section 26 of this act:

(1) At least two of the providers; and

(2) A combination of the providers whose combined market share represents at least 80 percent of the total market for the task workers within the bargaining unit.

For the purpose of complying with this paragraph, each of the providers is entitled to a number of votes for approval or disapproval that is proportional to the number of tasks completed by task workers who have contracted with the provider in the 2 immediately preceding calendar quarters.

3. For the purposes of subsection 1, a recommended agreement shall be deemed to be approved by an exclusive bargaining representative if an election concerning whether to approve the recommended agreement is held among all task workers within the bargaining unit who have completed at least 100 tasks in the immediately preceding calendar quarter and the approval receives a majority of the votes cast in the election.

4. A recommended agreement that has been approved pursuant to subsection 1 must be submitted to the Labor Commissioner as soon as practicable after approval.

Sec. 28. 1. If the providers and the exclusive bargaining representative have failed to reach a recommended agreement not less than 180 days after the date on which the exclusive bargaining representative submitted the notice to the Board pursuant to section 26 of this act, any party may declare the negotiations to be at an impasse by providing notice to the Board.

2. Upon receipt of a notice pursuant to subsection 1, the Board shall provide notice to the parties. The parties may, within 7 days after receipt of the notice, mutually agree upon the selection of a mediator. If the parties are unable to agree on the selection of a mediator within that period, the Board shall submit to the parties a list of potential mediators. Within 5 days after receipt of the list:

(a) The providers subject to the negotiations shall select one person to serve as a representative of all the providers;

(b) The exclusive bargaining representative shall select one person to serve as a representative of the exclusive bargaining representative; and

(c) The representatives selected pursuant to paragraphs (a) and (b) shall select the mediator from the list by alternatively striking one name until the name of only one mediator remains, who will be the mediator to hear the dispute. The representatives shall draw lots to determine which representative strikes the first name.

3. If the parties fail to select representatives and select a mediator pursuant to subsection 2

within 5 days after receipt of the list of potential mediators, the Board shall select a mediator to hear the dispute in question.

4. The mediator selected pursuant to subsection 2 shall bring the parties together as soon as possible and attempt to settle the dispute within 30 days after being notified of the mediator’s selection as mediator.

Sec. 29. 1. If the parties are unable to reach a recommended agreement within 30 days after the date on which the mediator is notified of being selected, any party may submit a request to the Board to refer the dispute to an arbitrator.

2. Upon receipt of a request submitted pursuant to subsection 1, the Board shall order an election to be conducted among all task workers in the bargaining unit represented by the exclusive bargaining representative who have completed at least 100 tasks in the immediately preceding calendar quarter. At the election, the Board shall order that each of the following be placed as a choice on the ballot for the election:

(a) A choice to submit the dispute to arbitration; and

(b) A choice to decertify the task worker organization as the exclusive bargaining representative for the bargaining unit.

3. If the choice to submit the dispute to arbitration receives a majority of the votes cast at the election, the Board shall order an arbitrator to be selected in accordance with section 30 of this act.

4. If the choice to submit to decertify the task worker organization as the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit receives a majority of the votes cast at the election:

(a) The task worker organization shall no longer be deemed to be the exclusive bargaining representative of the bargaining unit;

(b) The Board shall designate the bargaining unit as being without representation; and

(c) Any existing agreement or decision approved by the Labor Commissioner pursuant to section 32 of this act remains valid until the expiration of the agreement.

Sec. 30. 1. If the Board orders an arbitrator to be selected pursuant to section 29 of this act, the Board shall provide notice to the parties. The parties may, within 7 days after receipt of the notice, mutually agree upon the selection of an arbitrator. If the parties are unable to agree on the selection of an arbitrator within that period, the Board shall submit to the parties a list of potential arbitrators. Within 5 days after receipt of the list:

(a) The providers subject to the negotiations shall select one person to serve as a representative of all the providers;

(b) The exclusive bargaining representative shall select one person to serve as a representative of the exclusive bargaining representative; and

(c) The representatives selected pursuant to paragraphs (a) and (b) shall select the arbitrator from the list by alternatively striking one name until the name of only one arbitrator remains, who will be the arbitrator to hear the dispute. The representatives shall draw lots to determine which representative strikes the first name.

2. If the parties fail to select representatives and select an arbitrator pursuant to subsection 1 within 5 days after receipt of the list of potential arbitrators, the Board shall select an arbitrator to hear the dispute in question.

Sec. 31. 1. The arbitrator selected pursuant to section 30 of this act shall hold one or more hearings to receive information concerning the dispute. Each such hearing must be conducted in accordance with the provisions of this section and any other procedures and requirements established by the arbitrator.

2. At each hearing conducted pursuant to subsection 1:

(a) Each party may appear in person or by counsel or other representative designated by the party.

(b) Each party who is a provider who has contracted with at least 50 task workers in the bargaining unit in the immediately preceding calendar quarter and the exclusive bargaining representative may present, orally, in writing or both, statements of fact, supporting witnesses and other evidence and argument of their respective positions.

(c) Each party who is a provider who has contracted with less than 50 task workers in the bargaining unit in the immediately preceding calendar quarter may submit written evidence to the arbitrator.

(d) The arbitrator may administer oaths or affirmations, take testimony and issue and seek enforcement of a subpoena in the same manner as the Board pursuant to NRS 288.120 and, except as otherwise provided in subsection 5, the provisions of NRS 288.120 apply to any subpoena issued by the arbitrator.

(e) At the request of a party, a full and complete record of the hearing must be kept. The party requesting the record shall pay any costs associated with the keeping of the record. The record must be provided to all parties.

3. After the conclusion of the final hearing conducted pursuant to subsection 1, the arbitrator shall render a decision and report the decision to the parties. The decision must be in writing and include a statement specifying the reasons for the decision. In rendering the decision, the arbitrator shall consider the following criteria, as applicable:

(a) Whether the amount of compensation and benefits under consideration:

(1) Is sufficient compared to the cost of living; and

(2) Would allow a task worker who performs a sufficient number of tasks to support himself or herself without relying on any form of public assistance.

(b) Whether the mechanisms under consideration to deliver benefits to task workers are the most efficient means by which to provide such benefits.

(c) The financial ability of the providers to pay for compensation and benefits and the effect of such payments on the ability of providers to provide services through their task delivery platforms.

(d) Whether the mechanisms under consideration for the adjustment of grievances allow task workers a reasonable expectation of uninterrupted work and allow the providers to alter or terminate relationships with task workers if there is cause for such termination.

(e) Whether the compensation, benefits, terms and conditions of work are sufficient as compared to persons performing similar activities as part of the profession or occupation of the person.

4. A decision rendered pursuant to subsection 3 must be submitted to the Labor Commissioner as soon as practicable after it is rendered.

5. Except as otherwise provided in paragraph (e) of subsection 2, the providers subject to the negotiations and the exclusive bargaining representative shall each pay one-half of the cost of arbitration.

Sec. 32. 1. A recommended agreement approved pursuant to section 27 of this act or a decision rendered pursuant to section 31 of this act does not become effective until approved by the Labor Commissioner pursuant to this section.

2. The Labor Commissioner shall, for 30 days after the receipt of a recommended agreement or decision, allow the parties and affected task workers to submit written comments concerning the agreement or decision.

3. Except as otherwise provided in subsection 4, not later than 60 days after the conclusion of the 30-day period described in subsection 2, the Labor Commissioner shall:

(a) Approve the recommended agreement or decision without modification;

(b) Approve the recommended agreement or decision with such modifications as the Labor Commissioner deems necessary; or

(c) Reject the recommended agreement or decision.

4. If the Labor Commissioner intends to impose modifications pursuant to paragraph (b) of subsection 3, the Labor Commissioner shall provide notice to the parties and allow the parties and affected task workers, for 30 days after the date on which the notice is sent, to submit written comments concerning the proposed modifications.

5. In determining whether to approve, with or without modification, or reject a recommended agreement or decision pursuant to subsection 3, the Labor Commissioner shall consider the criteria set forth in subsection 3 of section 31 of this act.

6. If the Labor Commissioner approves an agreement or decision pursuant to paragraph

(a) or (b) of subsection 3, the Labor Commissioner shall provide written notice of the approval to the parties. The notice must include a date established by the Labor Commissioner on which the agreement or decision becomes effective and expires. The expiration date must be not more than 3 years after the date on which the agreement or decision becomes effective. An approved agreement or decision becomes binding on the parties on the effective date set forth in the notice and expires on the expiration date set forth in the notice.

7. The Labor Commissioner shall continuously monitor market conditions during any period in which an agreement or decision is effective pursuant to subsection 6. If the Labor Commissioner determines that market conditions require modification of the agreement or decision, the Labor Commissioner may modify the agreement or decision after providing notice to the parties and allowing the parties and affected task workers to submit written comments for 30 days after the date on which the notice is sent. A modification made pursuant to this subsection does not affect the effective date or expiration date of the agreement or decision.

Sec. 33. An agreement or decision approved by the Labor Commissioner pursuant to section 32 of this act does not preempt or supersede any statute or regulation setting forth labor standards that provide greater benefits or protections to a task worker.

Sec. 34. A person aggrieved by a determination by the Board pursuant to section 19, 20, 21 or 23 of this act, or a decision of the Labor Commissioner pursuant to section 32 of this act, is entitled to judicial review. A judicial review authorized pursuant to this subsection must be limited to a determination of whether the determination or decision was arbitrary, capricious or otherwise characterized by an abuse of discretion and must be conducted in accordance with the procedures set forth in chapter 233B of NRS for reviewing a final decision of an agency.

Sec. 35. The Board and the Labor Commissioner may adopt any regulations necessary to carry out their respective duties set forth in sections 13 to 35, inclusive, of this act.

Sec. 36. 1. In addition to any other requirements set forth by specific statute, a task delivery platform provider shall, for each task completed for a user through the task delivery platform of the provider, disclose to the user in plain language and in a conspicuous manner:

(a) The total cost to the user for the completion of the task by the task worker; and

(b) Each portion of the total cost described in paragraph (a) that is provided to:

(1) The task worker who completed the task; and

(2) The provider.

2. A provider shall regularly provide to each task worker with whom the provider has contracted a report that details the compensation paid to the task worker for the completion of tasks through the task delivery platform and any deductions from that compensation made by the provider.

Sec. 37. 1. A task delivery platform provider shall ensure that a task worker with whom the provider has contracted and who performs tasks through the task delivery platform of the provider is paid for each hour the task worker works in performing such tasks an amount that is not less than the minimum wage set forth in NRS 608.250.

2. If a task delivery platform provider violates the provisions of subsection 1, the task worker may, at any time within 2 years, bring a civil action against the employer.

3. If the task worker prevails in a civil action brought pursuant to subsection 2:

(a) The task worker is entitled to all remedies available under the law or in equity appropriate to remedy the violation by the provider which may include, without limitation, back pay, damages, reinstatement or injunctive relief; and

(b) The court must award the task worker reasonable attorney’s fees and costs.

Sec. 38. 1. A contract between a task worker and a task delivery platform provider to allow the task worker to receive connections to users in need of the performance of a specific task through the task delivery platform of the provider must:

(a) Be written in a clear and comprehensible language that is understandable to an ordinary layperson.

(b) Contain clear, conspicuous and accurate details of:

(1) The rates of pay provided to the task worker for the performance of tasks through the platform;

(2) Any fees charged by the provider to the task worker;

(3) The conditions under which the task worker may be removed from the platform; and

(4) The procedures for the resolution of disputes.

(c) Not be in a form which requires the task worker to manifest his or her assent to the contract by clicking a button or pop-up window that reads “ok” or “agree” or conveys a similar message.

2. A provider shall not engage in any practice with respect to a contract described in subsection 1 that is deceptive or that encourages misrepresentation or misunderstanding of the contract.
