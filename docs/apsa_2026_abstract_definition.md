Artificial intelligence bills surged in U.S. state legislatures in 2025, yet lawmakers still lack a shared statutory definition of AI. This paper asks whether early definitional language shapes subsequent bills and whether diffusion follows partisan, economic, or geographic lines. I argue that definition text is not random boilerplate: first movers anchor later language, and states cluster into distinct definitional communities linked to partisan control and regional economies. Using a corpus of 1,200+ state AI bills, I deploy a large language model pipeline to extract exact AI definitions and construct a semantic similarity network. I then test first-mover and homophily dynamics with weighted stochastic block models and GERGM, incorporating bill timing, party composition, and state economic indicators. Preliminary results show Democrats introduce more AI bills and include definitions more often (47% vs. 40%), and definition inclusion varies across regions beyond raw introduction counts. I expect to find significant block structure, with earlier bills and economically similar states seeding definitional templates. The findings illuminate how definitional choices diffuse, revealing which actors set the regulatory scope of AI and how partisan and regional contexts channel semantic convergence.
