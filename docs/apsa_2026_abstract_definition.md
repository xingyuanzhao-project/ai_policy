How States Define AI: LLM Extraction and Network Analysis of Legislation

How do states define artificial intelligence? This is not merely a semantic question; it determines who faces regulation and who is exempt. Yet across 1,200+ AI bills introduced in U.S. state legislatures in 2025, no shared definition has emerged.

When tracking the patterns of these definitions, keyword-based methods might struggle as AI definitions are buried within lengthy legal text and vary widely in structure and terminology. This paper shows that LLM-based extraction combined with network analysis is capable of systematically and accurately locating and tracing how definitional language diffuses across state legislatures. Unlike prior work that tracks bill adoption, we trace the semantic content itself to examine how the meaning of "AI" travels across state lines.

We propose a large language model pipeline that reads full legislation text for each bill, prompts the model to identify explicit statutory definitions, and extracts the exact definition language without paraphrasing. Applying this pipeline to 1,200+ state AI bills, we construct a semantic similarity network from the extracted definitions. We then apply weighted stochastic block models and GERGM to test whether introduction timing, party composition, and economic indicators predict block membership.

The approach that combines LLM-based extraction with network analysis enables researchers to systematically extract and compare statutory AI definitions at scale from lengthy and varying legal text where keyword methods fail, detecting semantic patterns that may shape the emerging policy landscape.
